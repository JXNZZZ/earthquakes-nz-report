Pt. 01

Spatial Distribution of Earthquakes in New Zealand

Earthquakes in Aotearoa New Zealand show a clear linear and clustered spatial distribution, concentrated almost entirely along the boundary between the Pacific and Australian Plates.1 The map displays a tightly packed chain of earthquake symbols forming a long, narrow corridor that runs from the Fiordland region, up the Alpine Fault, across Wellington, and along the North Island's east coast.2 This creates a strong southwest–northeast alignment, where earthquakes form dense clusters rather than being spread evenly across the country.

In the South Island, the pattern is dominated by the Alpine Fault, which appears on the map as a continuous chain of earthquake symbols stretching hundreds of kilometres.3 This creates a corridor of high seismic activity on the western side of the island. Moving north, the map shows another intense cluster around Cook Strait and the Wellington region, where the symbols become tightly packed, indicating frequent and varied earthquake activity.4 Further up the east coast, especially near Gisborne and Hawke's Bay, the map again shows dense groupings of focal mechanisms, forming a continuous chain of activity along the plate boundary.5

In contrast, the map shows very few earthquake symbols in Northland, Southland, and the central interior of Otago, highlighting clear "low‑activity zones." These areas sit well away from the main seismic belt, reinforcing that New Zealand's earthquakes follow a distinct, uneven spatial pattern rather than occurring randomly across the landscape.

Pt. 02

Processes Causing Earthquakes in New Zealand

New Zealand experiences earthquakes because it lies on the active boundary between the Pacific Plate and the Indo‑Australian Plate, where different types of plate movement create stress in the crust.1 This plate interaction is the fundamental cause of the country's earthquake activity, and it directly shapes the spatial pattern described earlier.

At the plate boundary, the plates do not move smoothly. They lock, grind, and push, causing stress to accumulate until the rocks eventually rupture.2 However, the type of movement differs between the South and North Islands, and this creates different earthquake patterns.

In the South Island, the key process occurs along the Alpine Fault, a major transform boundary where the plates slide horizontally past each other.3 Because this fault is long, continuous, and well‑defined, stress builds along a single corridor. When the locked rocks slip, the energy release produces earthquakes that form a linear cluster stretching from Fiordland through the Southern Alps.4 The simplicity of the transform movement means earthquakes tend to align closely with the fault trace, creating a narrow, predictable band of activity.

In the North Island, the process is more complex. Here, the Pacific Plate is being forced beneath the Australian Plate in a subduction zone.5 As the descending plate bends and grinds downward, stress builds at multiple depths. This complexity produces dense clusters of earthquakes offshore and along the east coast, especially near Gisborne, Hawke's Bay, and Wairarapa. Subduction zones generate a wider spread of earthquake locations because stress accumulates both deep in the crust and near the surface, creating a broader and more varied pattern than the South Island's transform boundary.6

These contrasting processes create New Zealand's uneven spatial pattern. Transform movement produces a narrow corridor of earthquakes, while subduction produces wider, denser clusters. Areas far from both processes - such as Northland, Southland, and central Otago - remain quiet because little stress builds there.

Pt. 03

Impacts of Earthquakes on People and Places

One major social impact of the Christchurch earthquake (2011) was the large‑scale displacement of residents from damaged suburbs such as Bexley, Avonside, and Dallington.1 Over 7,000 homes were eventually placed in the Red Zone, meaning they were too damaged or unsafe to rebuild.2 This forced thousands of people to relocate, disrupting communities, schooling, and daily life. Many families experienced long‑term stress due to insurance delays and unstable living conditions.

A significant economic impact occurred in Christchurch's central city, where around 70% of buildings in the CBD were damaged or demolished.3 The total cost of rebuilding exceeded $40 billion, making it New Zealand's most expensive natural disaster.4 Businesses were forced to close or relocate, and the city's commercial activity shifted to temporary "pop‑up" areas such as Re:START Mall. This economic disruption lasted for years and reshaped the city's layout.

An important environmental impact was the widespread liquefaction across eastern Christchurch.5 Silt erupted from the ground, flooding streets, damaging pipes, and covering properties. In some neighbourhoods, liquefaction occurred repeatedly across multiple aftershocks, leaving land unstable and requiring extensive remediation. This environmental damage contributed directly to the decision to red‑zone large areas.

The Kaikōura earthquake (2016) also produced major environmental impacts, including over 80 landslides along State Highway 1 and the railway line.6 These landslides cut off Kaikōura for weeks, creating both environmental and economic consequences. The uplift of the coastline by up to 6 metres changed local marine habitats and permanently altered the landscape.7

Pt. 04

Significance of Earthquake Impacts

The displacement of residents in Christchurch was highly significant because it permanently changed the city's social geography. This impact occurred because liquefaction and ground failure made entire suburbs unsafe to live in. It was worse in eastern Christchurch due to softer soils and higher water tables. The long‑term significance is clear: communities were broken apart, schools closed or merged, and thousands of people never returned to their original neighbourhoods.

Economically, the most significant impact was the destruction of the CBD, because it reshaped Christchurch's commercial centre for more than a decade.8 This was more significant than short‑term business closures because it required a complete redesign of the city, affected employment, and cost tens of billions of dollars. The long‑term rebuilding effort continues today, showing how lasting this impact has been.

Environmentally, the Kaikōura landslides had a greater impact than the Christchurch liquefaction, because they cut off the region's only highway and railway, isolating the town for weeks. This occurred because the earthquake triggered slope failure along steep coastal cliffs. The significance was long‑lasting: transport routes took over a year to fully reopen, affecting tourism, freight, and local livelihoods.
